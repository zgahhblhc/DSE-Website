ICDM 2018
Industrial Recipe for Success:
3 Parts Data and 1 Part Algorithm Design

By: Tyler Derr (derrtyle@msu.edu)

As the title says, 3 parts data and 1 part algorithm design, these were the words of wisdom given to me when talking with Dr. Xindong Xu (the president of ICDM) and similar words from Dr. Feida Zhu (one of this year’s ICDM General Chairs), but perhaps this divide need some context. The final event on the last day was the “ICDM Panel: The Future of Data Mining – Frontiers, Opportunities, and Challenges”, in which Dr. Feida Zhu was the moderator and the expert panelists were Dr. Ramamohana (Rao) Kotagiri, Dr. Xindong Wu, Dr. Bhavani Thuraisingham, Dr. Dacheng Tao, and Dr. C. Mohan (listed from left to right in the above image). They discussed the future of data mining and answered a few questions from the audience, such as their thoughts on the role deep learning will play moving forward. However, one question that was posed by the moderator (Dr. Feida Zhu) was a question that related to the idea of whether currently today, if having a company, what should the balance be between focusing on obtaining/collecting “good” data as compared to spending more resources on researching new algorithm designs. Each of the panelists discussed their thoughts and just about everyone finally reasoned that both are necessary, except for Dr. Xindong Wu. He immediately said data is more important, and insisted that for both big industry along with start ups companies that they should be focusing on the data aspect of things. However, this seems to contradict the primary way of thought from perhaps a decade ago where everyone focused on developing the cutting edge algorithms (to ultimately obtain slightly better performance than the competition). I had wanted to ask the following question to the panel thereafter, but unfortunately they were out of time and only one other person was selected for a question: “If you had to assign a ratio between the importance of data and algorithms, what would it be?”, since none of the panelists included this for their answers previously.

I waited until after the closing remarks to find seek out Dr. Feida Zhu, to ask his opinion on this matter, since he first posed the question and is the founder of Symphony Protocol [1]. Thereafter I asked Dr. Xindong Wu if he could elaborate on the comments he made during the panel session. This is when he mentioned the ratio given here of 3 times the effort on data over the algorithm design. Dr. Xindong Wu and I further discussed why in today’s world the focus must shift towards data. My thoughts were basically that 1) essentially no competitor is going to share their data with you, 2) realizing that we can be “standing on the shoulders of giants” ~ Sir Isaac Newton, which in this context is to say that for the algorithm design it is quite reasonable to utilize the methodologies that are being developed in the top data mining and machine learning conferences (such as ICDM) and thus not requiring as much effort, since we do not need to start from scratch, 3) if the algorithms are more openly shared and the data is not, it seems only logical that the data should be more of the focus, plus it is a race to obtain the data first because once it is obtained it has the potential to be harnessed by the owner to outperform the competition, and finally 4) most are currently overlooking the fact that data collected from different cultures (i.e., sources/locations) can have vastly different insights and is likely to struggle when attempting to utilize data collected in one culture that was obtained in another. In regards to the last point, it is believed this is likely one of the reasons that Google allegedly has the modified censored search engine designed for China, that is allegedly codenamed Dragonfly (which the Google CEO Sundai Pichai has reportedly commented that things were “exploratory”, but the project has not been officially confirmed [2]). This is because although Google is collecting vast amount of information from around the world, in China they have roughly only 2% of the search market [3], while China has roughly 18% of the world population [4].

Another key point I’m wanting to share with everyone based on my experience at ICDM 2018 (and other conferences) is that you should always be curious and willing to ask questions. Although for some of us it might be out of our comfort zone to ask a question in front of the whole room, but with practice, it will becomes second nature, so you might as well start asking now (or at least by the next time your attending a conference).

Finally, wanting to highlight that participating in the PhD Forum is a great opportunity to share your ongoing dissertation topic while obtaining great feedback from your peers and experts who attend the session. This year we were lucky to have the session “Meeting with Mentors”, which consisted of, Dr. Panagiotis Papapetrou Dr. Bhavani Thuraisingham, and Dr. Xindong Wu, who in a panel format, answered our questions. I had asked them the question of first how to balance reading papers. More specifically, in that sometimes we need to go deeper to understand everything on very relevant topics to our own, but other times we are just seeking a higher level understanding of the methodology and the direction the community is focusing in at that time (i.e., finding the hot/trending topics) and furthermore, how they believe this changes (or perhaps needs to change) when transitioning from a PhD student to an Assistant Professor. If you’d like to hear more about their thoughts on this matter and also that of my own advisor, Dr. Jiliang Tang (who I have discussed the same topic with), I encourage you to drop me an email, or if you have any other thoughts of questions; I’d be happy to discuss with you.

Overall, I believe my time spent at ICDM 2018 this year was very fruitful, especially in terms of networking with the community (both my fellow PhD students and senior data mining researchers).
